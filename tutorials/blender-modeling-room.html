Modeling your first room

Blender tutorial · Part 2 · Beginner

In Part 1 we set up an isometric camera. Now it's time to build something for it to look at: a simple room with a floor, two walls, and a piece of furniture. Everything in this tutorial is built from basic shapes — cubes — scaled and positioned.

1. Clean up the default scene

  1. Select the default cube and delete it (press X or Delete).
  2. Keep the camera and light — we'll reuse them.

2. Build the floor

  1. Add a new cube: Add → Mesh → Cube (or press Shift + A).
  2. With the cube selected, press S then Z then type 0.05 and press Enter to flatten it into a thin slab.
  3. Press S then Shift + Z and type 4 to scale it up on the X and Y axes, making it a wide floor.
  4. Move it down slightly so its top sits at Z = 0 using G, Z, and typing a value.
Reminder: G = grab/move, R = rotate, S = scale. Typing X, Y or Z after these constrains the action to that axis — very useful for keeping things aligned.

3. Add two walls

We'll reuse the same trick — a scaled cube — to make flat walls.

  1. Add another cube (Shift + A → Mesh → Cube).
  2. Scale it down on one axis to make it thin (e.g. S, X, 0.05).
  3. Scale it up on the other two axes so it covers one edge of the floor (e.g. S, Shift + X, 4 — height and depth).
  4. Move it (G) so it sits flush against one edge of the floor, resting on top of it.
  5. Duplicate it with Shift + D, then press Escape to cancel the move (keeping it in place), and rotate the duplicate 90° with R, Z, 90 to create the second wall on an adjacent edge.

4. Add a simple piece of furniture

A small box makes a perfectly good stand-in for furniture (a table, a chest, a cabinet — whatever fits your scene).

  1. Add a new cube and scale it to a furniture-like shape, e.g. wider than it is tall.
  2. Move it to sit on top of the floor, away from the walls.
  3. Repeat with a couple more cubes of different sizes to build up a small scene — remember, simple shapes are enough at this stage.

5. Frame the shot

Switch to the camera view (Numpad 0) and adjust the camera's position and orthographic scale (from Part 1) until your floor, walls and furniture are nicely framed within the view.

Tip: select all your objects (A) and use View → Frame Selected (or Numpad .) to quickly center everything in the viewport while you work.

Next steps

You now have a basic room blocked out. In the next tutorial we'll add materials, set up lighting, and render the final image.
